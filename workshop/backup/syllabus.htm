Prerequisites

  • Students are expected to use their own laptops which have internet capability.
  • No previous experience with Linux or the applications used in this course is necessary.
  • Students should be prepared to spend at least 10-15 hours per week on this course.

Course Format for Each Week

All classes and laboratory sessions meet in the Chemistry Building, Room CHM T115.
  • Lecture Classes and Computer Laboratory Sessions: three hours per week;
    • Mondays: 2:00 PM - 3:15 PM
    • Tuesdays: 9:30 AM - 10:45 AM
  • Additional, non-scheduled time should be set aside for working on computer projects.

Projects

Projects will be based on various topics in inorganic, organometallic and bioinorganic chemistry and contain both lecture and computer components.

Reports for projects should be written in such a manner that someone with little knowledge of the area of Inorganic Chemistry, for example, but who knows science, can easily read them. Sample results should be included in the report to both demonstrate an understanding of the experimental results and to illustrate how the theory and calculations aid in understanding the experiments.

Reports should deal not only with understanding the results from various applications but also with interpreting these results in terms of the experimental observations. Significant attention should be given to the manner in which calculations are used to explain the underlying experimental chemistry. It is imperative to exploit calculations by extracting valuable insights as well as unraveling penetrating analyses to aid in understanding the experimental results.

Grading

The grade in this course will be mostly -- but not entirely -- determined by the projects which are assigned and handed in during the course of the semester. These projects will involve the integration of computer simulation, visualization, and data analysis with the underlying science. Specifically, those qualities of an acceptable project include:
  • providing a modicum of explanation for an underlying theoretical procedure, a crisp discussion of the calculations performed, and how these calculations aid in interpreting experimental results.
  • a convincing awareness for the quality and types of calculational procedures performed in attempting to verify experimental results.
  • a demonstrated competence in the use of application software.
Each assignment with have an open-ended component, and successful addressing of this aspect of each project will result in a significant enhancement of student grades. Thus, it may be possible on some assignments to achieve a high grade which can compensate for weak assignments.

Students should attempt to go beyond the routine assignment and contribute a novel investigation into a project.
