The co-operation between cells is a fundamental and widespread feature in tissues and organs. Signalling between cells by means of gap junctions ensures the ability of a cell to maintain internal equilibrium. Gap junctions are clusters of inter-cellular channels that directly couple groups of attached cells providing a pathway for small molecules and ions to co-ordinate and synchronize metabolic activities of the constituent cells.

The constituent proteins of gap junctions are called connexins and are important in understanding how they function and influence inter-cellular communications. For example, gap junctions provided by Connexin proteins are responsible for the synchronized contractions of heart muscle by the propagation of potentials.

Molecular Dynamics simulations are performed to reach a deeper understanding at a molecular level of connexin transmembrane proteins.

An example of one simulation involves placing a molecule in the aqueous phase and observe what happens as it follows a path through the Connexin pore. Starting with molecules very near the pore opening, Molecular Dynamics (MD) simulations are used to explore their pathway through the connexin channel, to determine the energetic barrier of entering the pore and to obtain the energetics of permeation.

It is known which molecules block the pore, or do not block. Those which permeate each type of channel include cyclodextrins (CDs), ABG-sugars, PA-sugars, dyes, some second messengers. For blockers, it would be important to determine side of the pore which is causing the block.

MD results for other permeants can be tested and confirmed experimentally. Sites of interaction proposed from MD studies can be tested experimentally using mutants. Pulling permeants through the pore might identify barriers or might give artifactual results if the time scale for MD simulation is not appropriate.
