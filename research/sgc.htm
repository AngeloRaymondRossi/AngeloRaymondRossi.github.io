This research focuses on signal transduction and more precisely, on unraveling the pathways that involve the two major second messengers, cyclic adenosine monophosphate (cAMP) and cyclic guanosine monophosphate (cGMP), and on understanding the mechanisms of modulation of soluble guanylyl cyclase (sGC), a heme containing enzyme that produces cGMP in response to activation by nitric oxide (NO).

Physiologically, the NO-cGMP signaling pathway is critically involved in vascular homeostasis via smooth muscle relaxation, in inhibition of platelet aggregation, and in synaptic plasticity in the nervous system. Pathophysiologically, dysfunction of this pathway is involved in the development of atherosclerosis, hypercholesterolemia, hypertension, erectile dysfunction and diabetes mellitus, which are characterized by impairment in vascular reactivity as well as neuropsychiatric disorders and retinal degeneration.

Almost two decades after NO was identified as the ligand for sGC activation, the mechanism for activation of sGC by NOand other compounds remains unclear, and the link between NO tolerance and desensitization of sGC is still intensely debated.

sGC is a heterodimer multi-domain signaling enzyme that contains the receptor-heme domain as shown below

The challenges involve the determination of the mechanism by which the signal, subsequent to NO binding, is transmitted from the receptor domain (heme binding domain) to the effector domain (catalytic domain) and the manner in which this transmission is effected in desensitization.

An understanding of the structural and molecular basis for the mechanism of regulation by sGC is sought by addressing three crucial questions:

  1. How is the NO signal transmitted from the receptor-heme domain to the catalytic-effector domain?
  2. How does sGC become unresponsive to NO following prolonge exposure or oxidative stress?
  3. Do endogenous modulators of sGC activity play a role in desensitization?

Understanding the mechanism of regulation by sGC and identifying regulatory molecules will be key to uncovering the molecular basis for developing compensatory therapies for some types of hypertension, atherosclerosis, erectile dysfunction and neurological disorders which affect million Americans.

Activation of sGC by Nitric Oxide (NO)

One basic problem entails understanding the activation of sGC by the NO. There are varying ideas on the mechanism: Does the NO react with an adjacent cysteinyl residue to form an S-nitrosothiol?; Does the NO bind to the HEME first to form a rigid template and then react with the cysteine? Does the NO directly bond to the cysteine? Are two NOs ineeded to allow the mechanism for activation?
